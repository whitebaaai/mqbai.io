Land of Many Lands

Reorganizing the Coasts of Fragility in the Pacific Ring of Fire: Following the Trace of Sulfur Movements

Early scientific reports in 2007 by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) estimated a sea-level rise of under 60 centimeters by 2100. This figure does not include the possibility of rapid ice flow from Greenland or Antarctica into the sea, which could substantially increase the sea level by up to 65 meters. The Paris Agreement of 2015, with its goal "to limit global warming to well below 2, preferably to 1.5 degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels," has underscored the urgency of pushing for economic and social transformations to decarbonize.

The North American west coast, along the Pacific Ring of Fire, hosts clusters of large metropolitan areas operating across complex intercontinental industrial networks. This network constitutes the emergent notion of the Pacific Ring of Fire. The rise in sea levels poses threats to Canada’s largest western commercial port in British Columbia, the Silicon Forest in Oregon, Hollywood in California, and Silicon Valley in the Bay Area. As a sample of the entire Ring of Fire, these metropolitan regions represent a complex web of fragility; damage to these networks could have cascading effects on other intertwined global industrial chains.

The project is an architectural curation of knowledge aimed at establishing a space for negotiations and collaborative opportunities among diverse interest groups. These cultural cohabitation hubs would foster collaborations among local governments, foreign immigrants, First Nations, and emerging technological elites. The establishment of these cohabitation hubs within the cross-border region of Vancouver-Seattle-Portland would address critical decisions regarding wetlands preservation, future urbanization developments, and the relocation of industrial lands. The project envisions a sustainable future by transforming a land of fragility into a land of possibilities to achieve decarbonization.

The Crisis of the Pacific Ring of Fire

Climate change triggers sea-level rise, submerging the Ring of Fire at the Pacific Ocean’s border. Along the circular contour of the volcanically active Pacific Ocean lie abundant industrial resources important to the Anthropocene.

Tectonic movements cause the collision and destruction of lithospheric plates under and around the Pacific Ocean. These continuous subduction zones, where volcanoes are created and earthquakes occur, push up indispensable deposits of natural nutrients and fertilizers, including sulfur, copper, palladium, nickel, and platinum, which support the development of the contemporary world.

The Pacific Ring of Fire has evolved from a geological term based on tectonic movements and mining sources into a geological extractivist concept. Industrial extraction infrastructures, logistic networks, and coastal metropolises are additions to the emerging concept of the neoliberal economy around the Pacific Ring of Fire.

The booming economy of industrial mining and its logistics has also transformed the urban cultures of coastal metropolises. Among the foreign diaspora in these major coastal metropolises are large Chinese communities, significantly contributing to the cities' growth with investments in land properties. Along the North American west coast, Vancouver is one such metropolis that has undergone economic transformations, along with Seattle, Portland, and the Bay Area. From an anonymous mill town to one of the world’s most advanced technological hubs, Vancouver has become an essential part of the evolved notion of the Pacific Ring of Fire.

While sea-level rise urgently threatens the coastal harbors along the Ring of Fire, it is necessary for metropolitan regions to reorganize mineral commodity supply chains to manage the emergent transformations.

Operational disruptions at the Vancouver port, Canada's sole international commercial port on the west coast handling mineral commodity imports and exports, will trigger a comprehensive reorientation of physical infrastructures and networks. Such physical transformations may incite conflicts over jurisdictions, ownership of unceded lands, cross-border policies and laws, and social structures. These conflicts could involve the First Nations, the Canadian government, the US government, and the Chinese community.

THE CRITICAL POINT

The Greater Vancouver As A Sample

Laying on the coast are large piles of sulfur, the by-product of crude oil awaits to be distributed to the rest of the world for fertilizer productions. As a critical point, the Vancouver harbor has been both a storage and a transitional hub of raw materials.

With soaring demands of contemporary food supplies rely greatly on sulfuric fertilizers, survival of large coastal infrastructures that store and transport global essential commodities have become urgently crucial.

CRITICAL POINTS ALONG THE AMERICAN WEST COAST

These critical focal points spread across international clusters of large metropolitan regions along the North American West Coast, including the Greater Vancouver in BC, Canada, Seattle, Portland, in Washington and Oregon states of the US.

Through the investigations into these infrastructural coastal areas via digital elevation models, the industrial networks of commodity trades and transportations are at stake against sea level rise as the most indispensable yet fragile properties to mankind.

THE COAST OF VULNERABILITIES

Within the same geographic area also lies a series of natural threats, including active volcanic activity. Eruptions of Mount Baker and associated hazards, such as lava flows, flank failures, and spawning lahars or mudflows, are major concerns due to their ability to travel tens of kilometers from the source into populated areas without warning. A 2010 landslide and debris flow from Mount Meager, a volcano in British Columbia, Canada, exemplify the significant threat posed by spontaneous flank failures.

TRANSFORMED BY SULPHUR

Fort McMurray, Alberta, Canada

Prospered by the international economy of extractions, the metropolitan cluster of Greater Vancouver in Canada and Seattle-Tacoma in the US resembles the Pacific Ring of Fire, transforming into a gigantic anthropogenic urban environment.

Intercontinental demands drive the speed of extractions, which is reflected through the booming production of sulphur, the accelerated manipulations of the natural environment, and the decay of water bodies on the Athabasca Oilsand in Fort McMurray, ALberta, Canada. The demands of prosperity from elsewhere would fasten the development of the landscape of extractions here.

THE INSPECTIONS

The Greater Vancouver, a Sample of the Critical Points.

The Greater Vancouver is sectioned as a sample of the greater metropolitan complex of fragilities and knowledge across the western Canadian and US border, where the natural reserves and industrial sites occupied by a highly unique and complex system of knowledge are at urgent stake under the 2-m sea-level rise.

Wetland Vulnerabilities

The GIS map of the Greater Vancouver region highlights areas of wetland vulnerability, with blue indicating lower vulnerability and natural water bodies, while pink or orange signifies higher vulnerability in urbanized or developed regions.

The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)

By measuring the difference between near-infrared (which vegetation strongly reflects) and red light (which vegetation absorbs), this NDVI map shows vegetation and non-vegetation areas with green and purple hues. The green areas indicate healthy, dense vegetation, while the purple areas represent water bodies or non-vegetated surfaces.

The Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI)

The NDWI map highlights significant water bodies in blue, while urban and drier areas are shown in yellow to red, providing a clear distinction between water-rich and water-deficient regions in Greater Vancouver.

Industrial Land Occupancy

Out of the 21% cropland and the 6% industrial land coverage of Greater Vancouver, there are multiple environmental hazards in the water bodies and the atmosphere caused by industrial and agricultural discharges from sites of pulp mills, landfills, underground storage, and mining.

REAL ESTATE BOOM AMID SEA LEVEL RISE IN GREATER VANCOUVER

The Foreign Diasporas

Since 2008, Chinese outward real estate investment has increased more than 200-fold, reaching approximately US$33.7 billion by mid-2014. Favorable currency exchange rates and efforts to internationalize the renminbi have motivated Chinese investors to pursue opportunities abroad.

On the policy side, the Chinese government has taken action over the years to encourage outward investment as part of its "Going Global" agenda. In October 2014, the Chinese Ministry of Commerce adopted revised "Measures for Foreign Investment Management," which permitted domestic firms to invest in non-sensitive countries and sectors without prior government approval; previously, such approval was required for all overseas investments over US$100 million.

In May 2015, China announced the Qualified Domestic Individual Investor (QDII2) program, a pilot set of regulations that would allow individuals with at least one million renminbi in financial assets to invest directly in overseas financial assets and real estate. As a result of uncertainties from China’s slowing economy and political reform agenda, investors tend to diversify their holdings by investing overseas.

With the anticipated sea-level-rise, the land becomes unstoppably fragile due to the expansion of the real-estate developments that would leave huge footprints of contaminations in areas to be submerged underwater. These potential crises not only would affect the interests of future investors but also add significant and intangible risks to the environmental health.

CONFLICTS OVER THE UNCEDED LANDS

The First Nations

Geographically, the Greater Vancouver Region is also unceded land and part of the traditional territories of the Salish peoples, who view and manage the land differently from the modern divisions along the Canada and American West Coast, including areas around Vancouver, Victoria, Seattle, and Portland. This serves as evidence of modern conflicts between the Salish peoples and the Canadian-American governments over land rights, emphasizing the ongoing disputes regarding land use, resource management, and recognition of sovereignty.

From 2019 to 2023, The Cowichan First Nation has been involved in a dispute over land and fishing rights in the Greater Vancouver area, particularly due to land reclamation projects that expand urban areas into the sea. These expansions have encroached on traditional fishing areas, affecting the Cowichan's ability to exercise their rights to fish, which are protected under Canadian law. Specifically, land reclamation activities have added approximately 1,500 hectares of new land, impacting critical marine habitats and traditional fishing grounds. The Cowichan First Nation has raised concerns that these projects infringe upon their treaty rights and threaten the sustainability of marine resources, leading to ongoing legal and political challenges.

CONSTRUCTING THE COHABITATION HUBS

Turning The Space Of Fragility Into One Of Collaboration

To overturn the extractive system reliant on intrusive interventions that harms the environment, it is crucial to leverage local knowledge of the land to transform its space of fragility into space of collaborations.
